My PhD Thesis

On this site I will collect information about my thesis that might be of interest to my committee members and opponent.


The format of a PhD thesis at Chalmers is somewhat unusual. The student writes what is called a kappa. This is a section at the beginning of the thesis that presents an introduction, relevant background, and conclusions. After this, the papers the student has chosen to include in their thesis follow one by one. I am in the process of writing my kappa right now, so I don't yet have a draft to put here. However, I have picked my thesis statement. The grand question that has constantly been in the back of my head during my PhD is


Can a single high-level Haskell program be written once and automatically partitioned, compiled, and deployed across a heterogeneous network of devices, consisting of MCUs, TEEs, cloud nodes, with the type system statically enforcing the safety properties of each node?

I have worked towards this vision, and if I narrow down precisely what my thesis has done to answer this question, my thesis statement becomes

This thesis investigates the feasibility of using Haskell's abstractions and compiler infrastructure to target execution environments the language was not necessarily intended for, such as resource-constrained microcontrollers, trusted execution environments, and distributed systems, and demonstrates that tools such as EDSLs, type-directed partitioning, and graph serialisation are sufficient to bridge the gap.

I will put a PDF of my thesis here as it becomes more ready, but for now, I reference the papers that I intend to include in the thesis.



  • (PDF) CloudMicroHaskell: Direct-Style Distributed Haskell via Runtime Graph Serialisation

    Robert Krook, Lennart Augustsson
    In Submission to the Haskell Symposium 2026

    The version of the paper linked here is the one submitted to the Haskell Symposium. Since the submission, the authors have worked more on the paper and as a result the evaluation has substantially changed. For example, the time taken to spawn a process has been reduced to 60 us compared to the 2100 us reported in the paper. Additionally, there is another case study presented where a heterogeneous network of devices run nodes (laptops and MCUs). This new case study is intended to give a better answer to "why MicroHaskell" than the one presented in the paper. The link here will be updated to lead to the new version of the paper as soon as possible, but no later than the camera-ready deadline of the Haskell Symposium.
